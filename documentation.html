Project Documentation

Design Process

As an avid reader and self proclaimed book enthusiast, I wanted to do the final project with a data set relating to books in some way.
After a few searches I found the Goodreads-books data set on Kaggle. This data set is a comprehensive list of all the books on the
Goodreads app, along with the books' respective author(s), average rating, isbn, number of pages, number of ratings, and number of
text ratings. After viewing the data set the question I decided to pose as my research question is: What books have the most ratings
and how do the average ratings compare between the books with the most ratings?

My first design to answer this question was a bar plot with the title of the book on the x-axis and the average rating on the y-axis.
Then, when you hover over the individual bars a tooltip will display the exact average rating for that specific book. The overall
design stayed the same throughout the process of creating the visualization. However, I learned that I needed to be much more
specific in my design. On my first attempt, I filtered the data by books who have greater than 100,000 reviews. This lead to a very
messy graph with way too many bars for it to be easily understandable.

After texting out a few different values for the minimum number of ratings I decided on 1,500,000 as this resulted in 18 books/bars
to be displayed. Now that the graph is legible, I realized that it would be nice if the number of ratings was also displayed in the tool
tip to provide more information to the viewer.

At this point the visualization was clear and demonstrated my purpose, but it was still lacking interest and visual appeal. To remedy
this some color was added to the bars.

Lastly, to emphasize what bar the user is hovering over, I added a highlight feature to change the color of the bar when the curser
hovers over it.

Rationale of Design Choices

I chose to use a bar graph for this visualization as they are one of the most efficient channels for encoding both a quantitative
and a quantitative variable. For example, imagine if instead of a bar, a line was used. The viewer would still be able to discern
which books had higher or lower average ratings based on the vertices of the line, but it would be confusing why the separate
books would be connected. This is why using a bar graph in this situation is the best choice.

Color was added to each of the bars to make the graph more visually interesting. The purpose of this is to grab the attention
or curiosity of the view more than a black and white image would.

Lastly, an additional color or highlight element was added when hovering over a particular bar to make it more clear what
bar/book you were on when the tooltip information is displayed.

What I Discovered

This visualization answers both parts of my research question. The first part, what books have the most ratings, is answered
by the books that appear. That is, the books that are displayed along the x-axis are the 18 most rated books on the Goodreads
app. Utilizing the tooltip feature of the visualization allows the viewer to learn the number of ratings each book has. For
example, when hovering over Twilight the viewer learns that is has an average rating of 3.59 out of 4,597,666 ratings. Making
Twilight the most rated book on Goodreads.

The second part of my research question, how do the average ratings compare between the books with the most ratings, is answered
by viewing the graph. Visually, it is seen that the average rating of all the books is relatively high and there is not a whole lot of
variation between all of average ratings as they all range from about 3.5-4.5. Ultimately, all of the most rated books on Goodreads
have above average ratings.
